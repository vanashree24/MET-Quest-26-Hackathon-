# Furnace.exe — Decoding the Heat Gradient
### 3D Steady-State Thermal Simulation of a Muffle Furnace using the Finite Difference Method (FDM)
**Department of Materials Science and Engineering**

---

## Overview

Muffle furnaces are widely used for heat treatment (annealing, sintering), but they
frequently exhibit hidden thermal non-uniformities, hot zones near the back wall and
cold spots near the door, that cause parts to come out with inconsistent
microstructure/mechanical properties even at a fixed setpoint temperature.

This project numerically solves the 3D steady-state temperature field inside a
rectangular muffle furnace chamber to visualize and quantify these gradients, using
physically grounded models for wall conduction, radiation, and door heat loss.

## What the simulation models

- **Conduction** through a 3-layer insulated wall
- (refractory brick → ceramic fibre → steel shell), combined into a single series thermal resistance.
- **Linearized radiative heat transfer** at furnace operating temperature via an
  equivalent radiation heat transfer coefficient (Stefan–Boltzmann law linearized about
  the mean film temperature).
- **Convective + radiative heat loss through the door**, treated as a Robin (3rd-kind)
  boundary condition, since this is the dominant source of the "cold spot."
- **5 heated walls** (back, left, right, floor, ceiling) held at a fixed setpoint via a
  Dirichlet boundary condition.

## Governing equation

With heater elements embedded in the walls (not the gas volume), the interior gas
satisfies **Laplace's equation** at steady state:

```
∇²T = 0
```

solved on a uniform Cartesian grid using **central-difference FDM** with Gauss–Seidel
iteration (SOR framework, ω = 1.0 in this run), iterating until the maximum
node-to-node temperature change drops below 0.05 K.

## How to run

1. Open `METQUEST_PS_1_source_code_.ipynb` in Jupyter or Google Colab.
2. Run all cells in order (only `numpy` and `matplotlib` are required no external
   data or packages needed).
3. The solver typically converges in under 500 iterations (well under the 6000-iteration
   cap).
4. Two figures are generated and saved to the working directory:
   - `furnace_output.png` - 2D contour slices
   - `furnace_3d_viz.png` - 3D surface plots

| Convergence | Iteration 446, residual 0.0496 K |

**Key takeaways:**
- The back wall runs hottest (Dirichlet-enforced setpoint), while a pronounced cold
  zone extends roughly 8–10 cm inward from the door.
- The steepest gradients occur at the door-wall corners, where heat-loss pathways
  overlap ,the most likely sites for under-processed work-pieces.
- The door is the weakest thermal link: its effective resistance is roughly 8× lower
  than the insulated walls, so door sealing/insulation upgrades would give the largest
  uniformity improvement.
- Practical recommendation: keep work-pieces at least 10–15 cm from the door face.

## Key assumptions

- Steady-state only (no transient/heat-up behavior modeled).
- Conduction-dominated interior, natural convection of the internal gas is neglected.
- Radiation is linearized (small error, <3% at operating temperature, vs. the full T⁴ form).
- Heat generation is treated as uniformly distributed rather than from discrete elements.
- Material properties are constant, evaluated at mean operating temperature.
- No door-gap air infiltration (mass flow) is modeled, only surface heat transfer
  coefficients represent door losses.
